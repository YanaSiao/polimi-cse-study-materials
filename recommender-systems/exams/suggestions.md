# Subject Suggestions & Personal Experience

## Who Should Choose This Subject?

There are **no preliminary requirements** for this course.

The second half of the course is based on **machine learning concepts**, but it does **not require a deep understanding of ML** in order to master recommender system fundamentals. Taking an ML course beforehand saved me approximately **5–10 hours of study** for this subject.

This subject has **incredibly little theory** compared to many other subjects I took (such as AN2DL, ML, MIDA, etc.).

There are **two main groups of students** who may appreciate this course:

1. Those who want to **achieve a high mark** with a **low to medium time investment**
2. Those who want to **get hands-on experience** and continue a career in **applied AI**

This course optionally offers a challenge that I personally appreciated a lot:  
a **Kaggle-style competition** among the students, where you build recommender systems using an existing base.

If you love coding as much as I do, this challenge can easily consume **a significant amount of your free time**.  
For me, it was impossible to complete it gracefully while having **three other projects in parallel**.

**My recommendation:**  
If you aim to win the competition and gain the opportunity to participate in the **international competition** as part of the Politecnico team (and potentially go to the USA 🙂), try to have **no more than one additional project** during the semester.

## Exam Structure & Evaluation Options

There are **three possible evaluation paths**:

### Option 1 (Recommended if you like practice)
- Participate in the competition (**up to 27 points**)
- Take a **30-minute advanced theory exam**

### Option 2 (Classic pen and paper)
- **90-minute full theory exam** (**up to 26 points**)
- **90-minute coding exam** (pen and paper, **up to 5 points**)

### Option 3 (Not recommended)
- Full theory exam  
- Competition, with points **converted from a 27-point scale to a 5-point scale**

From my experience, **Option 3 is the worst** in terms of achievable points.

## How to Prepare for the Theory Exam

There is **not much theory** in this course.

The professor shares **notes from previous editions**, and reading and understanding those is **almost sufficient** to pass the exam.  
The new edition introduced only **minor topic updates**.

The theory exam is a **classic pen-and-paper exam**, and I do not have much additional advice beyond studying the provided notes carefully.

## How to Prepare for the Coding Exam

The method that worked **incredibly well for me** consists of **translating the code into human language or pseudocode**.

Conveniently, on the course GitHub page you can find **all the labs with code shared for Colab use**.

### Suggested preparation method:

1. Identify all **N main topics**
2. Extract key functions such as:
   - `fit()`
   - `recommend()`
   - metrics functions
   - loss functions
3. Rewrite each function **line by line** in:
   - human language, or  
   - pseudocode  
   (in a document or on paper)
4. Close the Colab notebook
5. Try to **rewrite the original code** using only your pseudocode
6. Repeat this process for all main functions
7. Train for a few days
8. After a few iterations, try to write the code **without looking at the pseudocode**

This approach builds both **understanding and memory**, which is crucial for a pen-and-paper coding exam.

## Personal Opinion on the Course

Professor Dacrema, in particular, is funny, makes jokes, and brings real-world examples.  
The lectures feel pleasant, and time passes quickly.

The unfortunate part *for me* was that the course felt **too light**, and I often left the classroom with the feeling that I could have learned more within the same time frame.

## Exercise Sessions & Labs

The exercise sessions are held by a **PhD student**, who explains the Colab notebooks **block by block**.

My recommendation depends on your learning style:

- If you prefer **live coding** and experimenting on your own:
  - Code at your own pace
  - Watch the recordings only when you encounter difficulties

- If you need **detailed explanations** and prefer being guided slowly, **line by line**:
  - Definitely attend the lab sessions

Both approaches work well — choose based on how you learn best.


## Final Thoughts

This course is a good choice if you:
- enjoy **practical work**
- want **flexibility in evaluation**
- are considering a **career pivot toward recommender systems**
