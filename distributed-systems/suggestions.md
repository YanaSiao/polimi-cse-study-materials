# Suggestions & Personal Experience

## Who Should Take This Course?

There are mainly **two groups of students** who should take this course.

The first group includes students who would like to **continue their careers in applied software engineering**.  
This course teaches, in detail, the theory behind how **parallel and distributed processes** work without breaking:
- timing guarantees
- synchronization
- system fault tolerance
- etc.

If this is your case, it is highly recommended to continue with the intended second part of the track:  
**Networked Software for Distributed Systems**, which focuses on the practical side using various middleware technologies.

The second group includes students who are **required to choose subjects from the TABA group**.

Passing this exam requires knowing **everything down to the last detail**.  
The exam format is standard, but achieving a **high mark is not easy**.
The subject is **semi-semester**, with lectures finishing in **October**, and the exam can already be taken in **November**.

## How to Prepare for the Exam

To pass the theory exam, you need to **attend almost all lectures** (with the exception of one lesson toward the end of the course).

You must:
- understand all distributed processes
- remember **algorithm complexity**
- know algorithms **step by step**
- understand **pros and cons**
- know **use cases** and limitations

I strongly recommend reviewing `distributed_systems_questions.md` **before starting each new topic**.  
This helps you build intuition about **topic priorities** and recurring exam themes.

Since the exam questions have **not changed significantly in the last 5–7 years**, you should focus primarily on those.

Once you have a general understanding of the full body of material and enter the focused exam preparation phase, I suggest the following:

- Allocate around **25 hours**
- Try to **answer all questions** in the list using the provided hints
- Use the hints to build **one coherent storyline** per topic that covers all key points

These professors do not care whether you write **two paragraphs or two book-sized reports**.  
What matters is that you include **all key details** from the course.

To achieve this, you should:
- keep a **clear structure** in mind
- use it as a set of **milestones** to maintain clarity
- then fill the structure with:
  - details
  - numbers
  - algorithms
  - side technologies


## Bonus Points

After passing the exam, you can obtain **up to 4 bonus points** by completing a **group project**.

To do this, you need to attend the **Simulation** lecture focused on **OmNeT++ technology**.

## Personal Opinion About the Course

First, I would like to give a **strong compliment to both professors**.

I was not particularly interested in the topic beforehand and initially took this course because it was semi-mandatory. However, the teaching style is **so clear and well structured** that by the second or third lecture I was genuinely surprised by how smoothly the material started to layer in my mind.

The professors are very open to clarifications and are willing to **repeat explanations as many times as needed** when students ask. Throughout the semester, I often felt as if they were genuinely **cheering for us** and supporting our learning process.

If I had to make the choice again, I would definitely **choose this subject again** for the high-quality teaching experience and the challenge it provides.

That said, I still consider this course **difficult** and one that requires **daily, consistent study** throughout the semester.
